Exploring “Home” Through South Asian Multimedia Art

The concept of "home" can carry many different meanings depending on one’s culture, identity, and lived experience. For South Asian American women in particular, the meaning of home is often shaped by intergenerational expectations and cultural influences from both the countries of their origin and their upbringing in the United States. These experiences are not only personal but also deeply political, as they often involve navigating conflicting values, coping with marginalization, and finding spaces for self-definition and expression. South Asian art and culture reflect a long history of using stories, symbols, and visuals to express identity, and that tradition continues today in the diaspora (The Metropolitan Museum of Art). At the same time, mainstream cultural narratives don’t always give space to South Asian American women's voices, which is why multimedia art offers a powerful platform for storytelling, activism, and reimagining “home.” Through a mix of symbolic imagery, personal narrative, and memory, artists use their work to challenge patriarchal and colonial legacies. In doing so, they redefine identity and highlight the political potential of art to transform cultural norms and amplify marginalized voices. By expanding the boundaries of who is seen, heard, and remembered, these women resist invisibility and reclaim power through creativity. They do this by crafting spaces of cultural survival, emotional truth, and radical imagination that reframe what home can mean. In doing so, they not only preserve their own histories but also reshape the broader cultural landscape, making space for more inclusive and representative narratives that challenge silence and foster connection. To fully understand the stakes of this creative resistance, we must first look at the historical forces that shaped the diasporic experience and continue to show through artistic expression. The history of South Asia—including colonization, religious diversity, and migration—has deeply shaped how diasporic communities see themselves. As the MET highlights, South Asian art has long used symbolism and storytelling to address political and spiritual themes (The Metropolitan Museum of Art). This legacy continues in diasporic art today, which often revisits key historical moments to process cultural memory and trauma. One such moment is the 1947 Partition, which refers to when British India was divided into the two countries of India and Pakistan due to political tensions, socio-economic factors, and religious differences between communities. This is considered a seismic event in South Asian history that led to mass displacement, loss, and identity fragmentation. Artists responded to this trauma by channeling their feelings of division, grief, and transformation into their work. For many, art became a way to both mourn and reimagine identity. In the Harvard panel discussion Partition’s Legacy: South Asian Art on the Line, art historians like Zehra Jumabhoy emphasize that contemporary South Asian artists—both in the subcontinent and the diaspora—continue to grapple with Partition’s enduring impact. They use visual language to express displacement, rupture, and emotional residue. As the panel notes, these works are not just about historical reflection, they are about healing, reclaiming agency, and challenging silences around political violence. Art thus becomes a living dialogue between the past and present. One example of this lasting influence appears in Leaping Bridges by Arpita Singh (see Fig. 1 in the appendix), a striking painting that captures the emotional contradictions of Partition, highlighting its deep personal pain, public trauma, and enduring aftermath. By using myth and memory to communicate pain, Singh not only reflects on the past but reveals how historical trauma continues to shape emotional landscapes across generations. It evokes the disorientation and emotional residue of Partition by creating a multigenerational connection and drawing from elements of the Ramayana, an ancient Hindu epic centered on themes of exile, loyalty, and the longing for return. By referencing the episode of the golden deer, an illusion that lures Sita, a goddess who is believed to be an incarnation of the Goddess Lakshmi, away and sets off a chain of separation. Singh draws a powerful parallel between mythic disruption and real-world dislocation, making the trauma of Partition feel both timeless and personal. It is important to note that referencing the Ramayana not only invokes a shared cultural touchstone but also reframes myth as a lens through which diasporic subjects can process feelings of separation, displacement, and the search for belonging. Rather than presenting a linear historical narrative, the piece layers myth, memory, and spatial imbalance to reflect the fragmented identities produced by forced migration. Singh’s visual language aligns with Zehra Jumabhoy and Gopinath’s ideas of diasporic art as a space for challenging dominant national histories and reimagining belonging through emotion and symbolism. The painting’s contrast between “my land” and “your land” powerfully dramatizes how imposed borders distort both geography and identity. As in Baig’s research, Singh’s work transforms personal experience into political expression, turning cultural memory into a form of resistance. This connection between history and identity is especially important for South Asian American women. For many of them, definitions of “home” are complicated by the tension between inherited cultural norms and American societal expectations. This dual pressure between tradition and modernity, between familial expectations and personal autonomy, produces both conflict and creativity. Baig’s oral history project offers a compelling account of how gender minorities and women navigate these tensions, revealing stories of cultural pressure, burnout, and identity negotiation. These narratives not only highlight the psychological toll of navigating conflicting cultural demands but also underscore the need for alternative platforms like multimedia art that empower women to reclaim their stories on their own terms. Multimedia art has become a particularly effective medium for exploring and expressing these layered identities. As a hybrid form that includes visual, digital, and written elements, it allows for nuanced self-representation. Gopinath’s idea of queer diasporic frameworks explains how these hybrid forms let artists resist standard ideas about gender, family, and the nation (Gopinath). Similarly, Laura Kina’s work situates Asian American multimedia art within longer trajectories of post-war cultural production and activism, showing how artists express belonging through cross-generational dialogue. One particularly resonant example of this artistic resistance is Drawing the Line: Indian Women Fight Back, edited by Priya Kuriyan. This anthology brings together comics and illustrations from Indian women artists who use visual narrative as a tool to address gender injustice and social inequality. Though focused on artists in India, the themes in this work resonate strongly with South Asian American creators. Kuriyan’s project shows how comics, long considered a trivial or playful medium, can become sites of radical expression, enabling women to visualize their lived experiences and resist dominant cultural norms. The anthology’s graphic style, use of metaphor, and narrative fragmentation mirror the approaches seen in diasporic art collectives like Kadak Collective. It also highlights the shared strategies across borders where South Asian women, regardless of location, use the visual language of comics to speak out against violence, repression, and silence. These works serve as firsthand examples of how a seemingly lighthearted medium can carry profound political weight and offer powerful tools for feminist resistance. Through powerful images and personal narratives in multimedia works, artists challenge dominant ideas of gender, identity, and home. These works often reflect internal struggles, like dealing with family expectations or figuring out identity, and turn them into acts of resistance. Baig shares how activists use storytelling to express their silence and emotional exhaustion in a way that pushes back. Watlington shows how queer and hybrid identities are portrayed through and centered in visual art to disrupt gender and race-based assumptions (Wallington). Kadak Collective’s Bystander Anthology uses comics and visual essays that spark discussions on themes like marriage, shame, domestic labor, and emotional repression to resist patriarchal norms (Davies). By transforming private pain into shared visual language, these works create space for collective recognition and healing, making the personal political and inviting viewers to question the norms they've internalized. Nair argues that graphic storytelling allows women artists to challenge gender expectations through visual techniques such as posture, panel layout, and composition. His analysis of Drawing the Line demonstrates how seemingly small artistic choices, such as how a woman stands or how confined a panel feels, can convey emotional resistance and challenge the conventional portrayal of femininity in media. In this way, the visual structure of these works becomes a tool for emotional and political expression, allowing artists to shape not just what is seen, but how it is felt and understood by the viewer. Another compelling example is the work of Chitra Ganesh, whose digital collages often combine elements of mythology, science fiction, and feminist critique. In her series The Unknowns, Ganesh reimagines female figures from Hindu iconography and South Asian comic books to highlight gender fluidity, agency, and rebellion. Her use of vivid colors and surreal compositions interrupts traditional representations of South Asian femininity and opens space for queer and feminist reinterpretation. In Ancestral Visit (2023), for instance, she depicts a female figure with multiple arms and hybrid features, blending the look of Western comic book heroines with Hindu deities and extraterrestrial beings. Her visual strategies encourage viewers to reconsider fixed ideas of gender, time, and cultural identity. Ganesh explores similar ideas in Eyes of Time, a mural at the Brooklyn Museum that combines drawing, painting, and sculpture. In a video about the piece, she explains that it focuses on “femininity and multiplicity,” drawing on museum objects and personal influences to imagine different forms of feminine power (view Fig 2 in Appendix). The central figure is inspired by the goddess Kali Ma, who represents time, change, and destruction in Indian mythology (Sarita). One figure has part of her face covered by a piece of the universe, which Ganesh says helps her look both inward and outward at the same time. Another figure is made of machinery and architecture, mixing futuristic elements with temple design to suggest a science-fiction version of divine power. Ganesh also talks about choosing objects that deal with fragmented bodies, mythology, and time, and she says that the present often reminds her of the science fiction she grew up with. By mixing old and new imagery, she shows how the past, present, and future can all exist in the same space. This connection between tradition and imagination also appears in Sultana’s Dream and Architects of the Future, where Ganesh uses speculative fiction to create worlds where South Asian women are central figures of power and transformation. By putting them in lead roles, she makes space for diasporic identities that are often left out of mainstream narratives. Instead of rejecting cultural tradition, she reshapes it, using familiar symbols to tell new stories. Her art gives South Asian women more ways to express identities that don’t always fit into existing categories. However, it’s also important to note that multimedia art is not only about individual stories but also about creating spaces for collective identity. Groups like Kadak and the South Asian Women’s Creative Collective build supportive communities that connect people across different countries and age groups. Through online zines and exhibitions, they expand access to feminist voices by providing a space for people to share their stories equally. Davies points out how the Kadak Collective’s artists “reclaim what counts as political” through comics and zines (Davies). Kavuri-Bauer adds how these artworks reframe the South Asian body as a political subject rather than an exotic object (Kavuri-Bauer). In the Her Stories project by the South Asian Women’s Creative Collective, they highlight first-person narratives and show how shared art spaces can bring people together and create solidarity (SAWCC). By centering marginalized voices and fostering dialogue across borders, these collectives challenge dominant narratives about who belongs and who gets to speak. Their collaborative approach transforms storytelling into a form of activism, where community-building itself becomes an act of resistance. This connects back to Baig’s dissertation, in which she interviews a diverse group of South Asian women to find commonalities in their experiences, as it allows for the multifaceted perspectives of South Asian women to be expressed in a curated platform. A key example of how the Kadak Collective has done this is their project Bystander Anthology, which features work from over 50 South Asian artists across 13 countries. This large-scale collaboration addresses themes such as domestic labor, mental health, caste privilege, and everyday gendered violence, using comics, essays, and visual storytelling to foster cross-border dialogue. The sheer diversity of contributors and topics underscores how diasporic identity is not singular but shaped by overlapping structures of class, caste, gender, and geography. By weaving together these distinct yet resonant voices, the anthology turns the act of witnessing into a shared political practice. In a world where dominant narratives often erase diasporic voices, these collective platforms serve as political spaces where cultural survival, feminist solidarity, and radical imagination converge. They help make clear that home is not just a physical place, it’s also a feeling of community and resistance. This hybridity not only reflects the diasporic experience but also pushes back against the idea that identity must be one-dimensional. By combining elements like illustration with oral storytelling, or blending archival photographs with personal memories, artists create layered identities that capture the complexity of living between cultures. Cultural theorist Homi Bhabha, in The Location of Culture, refers to this as the “third space,” a space where new meanings emerge through the interaction of different cultural influences. Within this space, South Asian American artists are able to explore their identities in ways that are flexible, constantly evolving, and grounded in their cultural backgrounds (Bhabha 55). This approach aligns closely with Gayatri Gopinath’s concept of the “queer diasporic” subject, where artists use hybrid forms not only to critique heteronormative and nationalist ideals but to imagine alternate ways of belonging (Gopinath). The blending of form becomes a mode of survival, allowing artists to articulate identities that don’t conform to dominant expectations of gender, race, or nationality. Santhi Kavuri-Bauer builds on this idea by showing how the South Asian body in art is reconfigured from an object of exoticism into an active political subject. Hybridity, then, is not just aesthetic—it is a strategy of resistance. It destabilizes fixed categories and allows for a more nuanced understanding of diasporic subjectivity. Similarly, Emily Watlington’s work on queer Asian American art reveals how hybridity allows artists to visually disrupt binary frameworks around gender and cultural identity. In works like those from Her Stories or The Bystander Anthology, this layering of media, narrative, and symbolism becomes a way to engage with generational memory, emotional labor, and personal autonomy. This process goes beyond simply expressing lived experience; it becomes a powerful method of resisting cultural erasure and reclaiming agency. Through multimedia storytelling, these artists make room for fragmented histories and marginalized voices, offering audiences new perspectives on what identity can mean. Their art functions as both a personal archive and a collective statement, demonstrating that identity is not fixed but is instead continually shaped by creativity, resilience, and the ongoing effort to be seen and heard. In doing so, they invite viewers not only to witness their stories but to reflect on the fluidity of their own identities and the possibilities that emerge when cultural boundaries are reimagined rather than reinforced. For many South Asian American women, creative expression becomes a compass in unfamiliar terrain, an emotional map through which they reconcile intergenerational legacies with present-day realities. This is because when living in a space shaped by both ancestral traditions and contemporary American society, these women often find themselves pulled in multiple directions as they are expected to honor inherited values while also pursuing personal growth and autonomy. This duality creates emotional and psychological tension that is difficult to articulate through conventional forms of discourse. Because of this, artistic expression becomes a powerful outlet. Multimedia storytelling, in particular, becomes an outlet for a layered dialogue: it not only critiques rigid cultural expectations but also creates opportunities to reconnect with family legacies on new terms. Gopinath’s analysis of queer diasporic subjects is especially useful in this context. She argues that art becomes a tool to “speak back” to heteronormative and nationalist ideals passed down through generations—ideals that often exclude or marginalize those who don’t conform to dominant cultural narratives (Gopinath). In other words, by reimagining identity through art, South Asian American women actively resist being confined to simplistic categories of tradition or assimilation. Similarly, Baig’s oral history project provides an intimate account of how South Asian American activists negotiate parental expectations, gendered pressures, and the emotional toll of trying to belong in both their families and society at large. These stories illustrate the emotional labor involved in carving out space for new identities, particularly when doing so means challenging the very foundations on which one’s upbringing is built. This often requires confronting deeply rooted cultural hierarchies, which is why many artists incorporate references to historical Indian literature, as seen in Figure 1. This both honors and critiques the narratives they’ve inherited with a progressive tone. In the Her Stories project by the South Asian Women’s Creative Collective, contributing artists use mixed media to explore these exact tensions. They layer personal memory with broader themes of generational conflict, expressing ambivalence around topics such as career choices, marriage, sexuality, and independence (SAWCC). These artworks reveal the subtle negotiations women engage in as they try to honor their cultural roots while also asserting their right to self-definition. Whether through visual metaphors, symbolic color palettes, or fragmented digital narratives, these artists show that healing and resistance can coexist—and that reclaiming “home” is as much about inner reconciliation as it is about social transformation. This blending of personal and cultural narratives aligns with the broader pattern seen across South Asian American multimedia art, where artists use hybrid forms not just to process identity, but to reshape it actively. Projects like Her Stories show that home isn’t inherited, it’s constructed, negotiated, and continually redefined through acts of creative self-expression. In doing so, these artists not only challenge patriarchal and cultural norms but also contribute to a growing visual archive of feminist resistance within the diaspora. This directly supports the broader claim that multimedia art is a critical tool for South Asian American women to reclaim identity, resist erasure, and imagine more expansive forms of belonging. Some may argue that multimedia art is too niche or not accessible to the general public. However, many of the works discussed in this paper, especially zines, digital comics, and online exhibitions, are created specifically to be widely accessible. As Davies and Baig both note, digital platforms help bypass traditional gatekeeping in the art world, reaching broader and more grassroots communities. The internet has allowed many of these artists to share their work without relying on galleries or institutional validation. Digital comics, zines, and online exhibitions are often free and widely circulated, giving artists direct access to community audiences. This makes multimedia art not only accessible but participatory as viewers can engage with, comment on, and share the work, turning it into a living dialogue. The online nature of these platforms helps build transnational networks of solidarity, making these artistic communities resilient and expansive. While some critics raise concerns that digitalization, particularly through technologies like NFTs and AI, may commercialize or depersonalize art, Seda Eğridere and Tülin Ok argue that digital platforms also offer new creative freedoms and ways to redefine artistic value and authorship. Although these technologies can introduce market-driven dynamics, they also expand who gets to make and share art (Eğridere and Ok). In the context of South Asian American multimedia art, digital tools are used not for profit but to amplify marginalized voices and foster community connection. The online nature of these platforms helps build transnational networks of solidarity, making these artistic communities resilient and expansive. Another potential critique is that this topic is only relevant to South Asian communities. While this paper centers South Asian American women, the themes, navigating cultural tension, resisting gender roles, and rethinking belonging are relevant to many immigrant and marginalized groups. By looking closely at one community’s experiences, we gain insights that resonate more broadly and can reflect on similar patterns elsewhere. Of course, the South Asian diaspora itself is not monolithic. Artists come from different religions, languages, regions, and upbringings, so their experiences and world views vary. Still, the examples highlighted in this paper reflect overlapping experiences and struggles that are worth exploring together (Priyanka). Overall, South Asian American women use multimedia art to share who they are and to push back against traditional gender roles and restrictive cultural expectations. Through illustrating their personal stories in comics, digital exhibitions, zines, and mixed media, they show that “home” can be flexible, multifaceted, and ever-evolving. It’s not a fixed idea rooted in one geography or tradition, but rather something shaped by many cultures, memories, and experiences. Their work builds community, sparks dialogue, and helps bridge generational divides, while also contributing to larger conversations about race, gender, and belonging in America today. Artistic practice invites reflection and re-education by encouraging viewers to unlearn dominant narratives and listen instead to lived experience, emotion, and memory. It’s more than just aesthetics and is a form of knowledge production, a space for healing, and a method of activism. It gives marginalized voices a platform to speak their truth and to be seen on their own terms. This can help us see the world and each other in new ways while imagining new forms of belonging within ourselves and our communities. More than just a personal outlet, this art becomes a collective archive of experience. It is a living, breathing record of how South Asian American women navigate the challenges of belonging, displacement, and identity formation. It pushes back against silence and invisibility and offers alternate visions of identity that are dynamic, resistant, and evolving. These narratives do not just critique, but they also build, envision, and dream. As more artists continue to experiment with hybrid forms and digital platforms, they expand the possibilities of what art can do and who it can reach. Recognizing and supporting these creative efforts is essential to fostering inclusive cultural dialogue and imagining a future where everyone’s story can be seen, heard, and valued, not just within the South Asian diaspora but within a broader global movement for equity and expression.
