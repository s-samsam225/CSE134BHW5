Exploring “Home” Through South Asian Multimedia Art

The concept of "home" can carry many different meanings depending on one’s culture, identity, and lived experience. For South Asian American women in particular, the meaning of home is often shaped by intergenerational expectations and cultural influences from both the countries of their origin and their upbringing in the United States. These experiences are not only personal but also deeply political, as they often involve navigating conflicting values, coping with marginalization, and finding spaces for self-definition and expression. South Asian art and culture reflect a long history of using stories, symbols, and visuals to express identity, and that tradition continues today in the diaspora (The Metropolitan Museum of Art). At the same time, mainstream cultural narratives don’t always give space to South Asian American women's voices, which is why multimedia art offers a powerful platform for storytelling, activism, and reimagining “home.” Through a mix of symbolic imagery, personal narrative, and memory, artists use their work to challenge patriarchal and colonial legacies. In doing so, they redefine identity and highlight the political potential of art to transform cultural norms and amplify marginalized voices. By expanding the boundaries of who is seen, heard, and remembered, these women resist invisibility and reclaim power through creativity. They do this by crafting spaces of cultural survival, emotional truth, and radical imagination that reframe what home can mean. In doing so, they not only preserve their own histories but also reshape the broader cultural landscape, making space for more inclusive and representative narratives that challenge silence and foster connection.
